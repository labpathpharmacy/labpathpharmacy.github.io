Step 3 | Crime Scene Chemistry in pharmacy: The Cool Blue Light of Luminol


1. Objective/Aim


In this experiment, the objective is to investigate if luminol reaction/test works for both dried and wet blood. It’s also able to apply forensic science, which uses science for criminal investigations.


2. Background / Introduction


Some chemical reactions show lights. But, why do they glow and where does light come from? This type of reaction has wide applications. For instance, glow sticks also use this principle. This process is called chemiluminescence which produces light by using chemical energy. Specifically, light is generated in the process of returning to the ground state of materials after they gain higher energy and turn into an excited state. To turn a substance to an excited state, there should be enough energy to make changes in electron arrangement. After a certain time, the substance will return to its ground state, emitting light energy, which enables us to detect light.


Application of this principle isn’t limited to only for entertaining usages like glow sticks. In fact, they actually help detectives/police to find criminals. The most well-known application in criminology is luminol testing, which uses this light detection for blood. This helps detectives to find unclear blood traits, and even guess how the crime occurred by analyzing the direction and general shape of blood traits. Luminol has been a powerful tool over time. For example, many different criminal cases such as the Gangadhar Chadchan Case in India, Laura Houghteling case in Washington D.C., luminol provided strong evidence to find the criminals.


The picture above represents luminol testing. How this reaction is used for blood detection is because of the slow reaction rate of the reaction shown above. In fact, as the following image demonstrates, blood can be a catalyst.

As oxygen particles from hydrogen peroxide collide to luminal dianion, it becomes 5-aminophthalic acid, which will be in a still excited state. As the substance relaxes to ground state, the excess energy appears as a photon, which is visible as blue light.

3. Materials / Equipment


For conducting the experiment, experimental materials and equipment are required; the materials are following this:


Safety Equipments: Safety Goggles and Gloves + Lab coat if needed


Reagents being used: Distilled water, Hydrogen peroxide solution(3%), Luminol test solution


Catalyst: Blood


Experimental Equipment: Beakers, graduated cylinder, funnel, stirring rod


4. Method / Procedure


a. Prepare hydrogen peroxide and luminol solution


b. Put 2ml of Hydrogen Peroxide to luminol Solution


c. Darken the environment completely.


d. Prepare Blood


e. Spray luminol onto the paper.


f. Observe blue fluorescence where dried blood and wet blood are.


5. Result/ Discussion(Analysis)/ Conclusion


When luminol solution is placed and contact with the dried bloodstain, blue light was also shown, although the strength of the light was weaker and the duration was also shorter compared to that of wet blood. This might be because of difference in amount of blood, which is directly associated with catalyst in this reaction. Influence in catalyst changes the reaction rate, and in this experiment, also the strength of light. There wasn't a light that produced in areas where blood was not present. Nevertheless, these findings does not changes the conclusion where the luminol reaction occurs in the presence of blood whether it is wet or dried. In other words, the hypothesis was supported. In the luminol experiment, it successfully produced a blue glow for both dried and wet blood, confirming that luminol can detect the blood regardless of its condition. This result demonstrates the effectiveness of chemiluminescence in forensic science with explanation of background theory.


Although the light emission was temporary and required a dark environment, the reaction was clearly observable. Overall, the experiment showed how luminol can be a useful presumptive blood testing tool in crime scene investigations.
