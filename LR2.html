Step 2 | Acid-Base Titration in Pharmacy: Measuring pH and Observing Neutralization Reaction


1. Objective/Aim


The purpose of this experiment is to learn how acids and bases react with each other through a titration process. By measuring pH changes, finding the point where the acid and base completely neutralize each other is possible. This experiment helps to understand how pH works in chemical reactions and how this concept can be applied in pharmacy, such as when analyzing or preparing drug solutions.


2. Background / Introduction


This experiment uses Arrhenius definition of acid and base, which states that Arrhenius acid is a compound that increases the H+ concentration in aqueous solution, and Arrhenius base is a compound that increases the OH- concentration in aqueous solution. It also states that when Arrhenius acid is dissolved in water the pH would be lower than 7, and when Arrhenius base is dissolved in water the pH would be higher than 7.


We have to know that when an acidic substance and a basic substance are mixed, a neutralization reaction occurs. In general, neutralization reactions among acid and base reactions refer to Arrhenius' definition of acids and bases. HCl + NaOH → NaCl +H2O is a great example of a neutralization. There would be heat generated during the neutralization titration process. This occurs when the H+ ion of the acid and the OH- ion of the base meet to produce water (H2O). The more water molecules produced as a result of neutralization titration, the more heat of neutralization can be confirmed. So what actually is neutralization titration? Neutralization titration is the process of quantifying the concentration of a solution through acid-base neutralization reactions. By using this background information, we will learn, and experiment about how acids and bases react with each other through a neutralization titration process.


3. Materials/ Equipment


For conducting the experiment, experimental materials and equipment are required; the materials are following this:


Safety Equipment: lab coat, lab safety goggles, and the latex gloves.


Reagents to be used : hydrochloric acid (HCl) of 0.1 M, Sodium hydroxide solution of 0.1 M, phenolphthalein solution, vinegar, distilled water.


Equipment to be used : pipette filler, pipette, stirrer, clamp, graduated cylinder, beaker, retriever, magnetic stirrer bar, triangular flask, funnel, stand, burette.


4. Experiment Method & Procedure


How to use Pipettes and Burette


a.Pippette

To move exactly to the last drop, remove the pipette filler and hold the tip of the pipette with one hand, wrap the pipette torso with the other hand, and use your body temperature to let the liquid flow completely.


b. Burette

Before the experiment, rinse thoroughly with water before using it. Check the condition of the cock and adjust it so that the liquid can flow at a constant speed.


Procedure


a. Take 10 mL of 0.1 M dilute hydrochloric acid using pipettes and pipette fillers, and then put it in a triangular flask.


b. Put 3 to 4 drops of the magnetic bar and phenolphthalein solution together in a triangular flask.


c. Put 0.1M aqueous sodium hydroxide solution into the locked buret using a funnel. Also adjust the end of the burette containing air so it can be filled.


d. Fit the triangular flask well with the end of the burette and turn on the stirrer.


e. Open the cock and drop it into a triangular flask until a total of 5 ml, 1 ml of aqueous sodium hydroxide solution for each time, and observe the color of the mixed solution.


f. Subsequently, add 1 ml of aqueous sodium hydroxide solution again for each time to the triangular flask, until a total of 9 ml is dropped and observe the color of the mixed solution. Then, add the remaining 1 ml aqueous sodium hydroxide solution drop by drop and observe the color of the mixed solution.


g. After that, add a little more aqueous sodium hydroxide solution to observe the color of the mixed solution.


h. Adjust the cock so that the volume of the aqueous sodium hydroxide solution is 15 ml in total, add 1 ml each, and then observe the color of the mixed solution.


5. Discussion

After conducting the experiment, the hypothesis was rejected. Instead of 5ml of acid, 10ml of acid was needed to change the color. However, the color change was observable, and it has successfully changed to pink as several drops of sodium hydroxide solution were added. In addition to observing the color change to slight pink, the experiment went beyond it, and was able to recognize a vibrant pink after putting more base. This change in color can be explained by the Acid-Base Titration, which was mentioned in the “Background / Introduction” section.
